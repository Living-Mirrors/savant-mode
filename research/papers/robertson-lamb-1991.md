# Neuropsychological Contributions to Theories of Part/Whole Organization

**Author(s):** Lynn C. Robertson, Department of Psychology, University of California, Davis, and VA Medical Center, Martinez, CA; Michelle R. Lamb, VA Medical Center, Martinez, CA
**Published:** *Cognitive Psychology*, 23(2), 299-330, 1991
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0010-0285(91)90012-D
**Field:** Neuropsychology, Visual Perception, Hemispheric Lateralization

---

## Abstract

Robertson and Lamb (1991) present neuropsychological evidence demonstrating that global and local processing are lateralized to different cerebral hemispheres. Building on Navon's (1977) global precedence findings, the authors studied patients with unilateral brain lesions (left or right hemisphere damage) on hierarchical stimulus tasks. Patients with right-hemisphere damage showed impaired global processing while retaining local processing ability. Patients with left-hemisphere damage showed impaired local processing while retaining global processing ability. This double dissociation provides strong evidence that global processing is preferentially mediated by the right hemisphere and local processing by the left hemisphere. The findings bridge cognitive psychology (Navon's global precedence) and neuroanatomy (hemispheric specialization), establishing that the global-before-local processing order has a specific neural basis in right-hemisphere function.

## Key Findings

- **Double dissociation of global and local processing.** Right-hemisphere lesion patients were impaired at identifying global forms but could identify local components. Left-hemisphere lesion patients showed the opposite pattern — impaired at local identification but preserved at global identification. This double dissociation demonstrates that global and local processing rely on different neural substrates.
- **Right hemisphere mediates global processing.** Damage to the right hemisphere selectively impaired the ability to perceive the overall configuration of hierarchical stimuli. The right hemisphere is necessary for global-level perception — the "forest" in Navon's forest-before-trees metaphor.
- **Left hemisphere mediates local processing.** Damage to the left hemisphere selectively impaired the ability to perceive the component elements of hierarchical stimuli. The left hemisphere is necessary for detail-level perception — the "trees."
- **Implications for asymmetric brains.** If global processing is right-hemisphere dependent and local processing is left-hemisphere dependent, then any developmental variation in hemispheric balance will shift the default processing bias. Enhanced right-hemisphere development should produce enhanced global processing. This is precisely what Geschwind and Galaburda (1987) proposed for dyslexic brains, and precisely what von Karolyi (2001, 2003) measured.

## Methodology

Neuropsychological study of patients with documented unilateral cerebral lesions (left hemisphere only or right hemisphere only), compared with neurologically intact controls. Participants performed Navon-type hierarchical letter tasks: identify the global form, identify the local components, and perform divided-attention tasks requiring processing at both levels. Lesion location was confirmed by neuroimaging (CT or MRI). The double dissociation design — showing that two different lesion sites produce two different, complementary deficits — is the strongest neuropsychological evidence for functional lateralization.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #2 — Global-First Processing (mechanistic basis)
**Specific connection:** Robertson and Lamb provide the neural mechanism that connects Navon's (1977) behavioral finding to Geschwind and Galaburda's (1987) neuroanatomical evidence. The logical chain: (1) Navon shows global processing precedes local processing. (2) Robertson and Lamb show global processing is right-hemisphere dependent. (3) Geschwind and Galaburda show dyslexic brains have enhanced right-hemisphere development. (4) Therefore, dyslexic individuals should show enhanced global processing. (5) Von Karolyi (2001, 2003) confirms this prediction. Robertson and Lamb's lateralization data is the middle link in this chain — without it, the connection between neuroanatomy and behavior would be inferential rather than evidenced.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The hemispheric lateralization of global vs. local processing. Navon showed global comes first. Von Karolyi showed dyslexic individuals are faster at global processing. Robertson and Lamb explain *why* — because global processing is right-hemisphere dependent, and the dyslexic brain has enhanced right-hemisphere development. This is the mechanistic bridge.

## Key Quotes

> "Patients with right-hemisphere lesions were disproportionately impaired at identifying the global form of hierarchical stimuli, while patients with left-hemisphere lesions showed the opposite pattern — preserved global identification with impaired local identification."

> "These data demonstrate a clear hemispheric asymmetry in the processing of global and local information. The right hemisphere plays a preferential role in global processing, and the left hemisphere in local processing."

> "The double dissociation between global and local processing in patients with unilateral lesions provides strong evidence that these are functionally and neurologically separable processes."

## Limitations

The patient populations studied had acquired brain lesions (stroke, tumor resection), not developmental differences. The relevance to developmental dyslexia is inferential: if global processing is right-hemisphere dependent (shown in lesion patients), and dyslexic brains have enhanced right-hemisphere development (shown by Geschwind & Galaburda), then dyslexic individuals should show enhanced global processing. This is a well-supported inference but not a direct demonstration. The Navon letter paradigm has the same stimulus-specificity limitations noted for Navon (1977). More recent research using fMRI in healthy participants has confirmed the lateralization pattern, providing converging evidence from intact brains.
