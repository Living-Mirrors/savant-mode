# Creativity and Dyslexia: A Systematic Review

**Authors:** Alexia Cancer, Department of Psychology, Universita Cattolica del Sacro Cuore, Milan; Alessandro Antonietti (senior author, same institution). Note: the systematic review draws on work by multiple researchers in the creativity-dyslexia intersection.
**Published:** Multiple studies synthesized, representative: Cancer, A., & Antonietti, A. (2020). "Creativity and dyslexia: Theoretical insights and empirical evidence." In *The Cambridge Handbook of Creativity and Personality Research*. See also: Cancer, A., Manzoli, S., & Antonietti, A. (2016). "The alleged link between creativity and dyslexia: Identifying the specific process in which dyslexic students excel." *Cogent Psychology*, 3(1), 1190309.
**Field:** Creativity Research, Cognitive Psychology, Dyslexia

---

## Abstract

Cancer and colleagues' systematic review examines the empirical evidence for enhanced creativity in dyslexic populations. Synthesizing studies that measured divergent thinking, creative production, and creative problem-solving in dyslexic vs. non-dyslexic participants, the review finds consistent evidence for enhanced divergent thinking in dyslexic individuals — particularly in spatial and visual domains — while noting that the advantage is not uniform across all creativity measures. The enhanced divergent thinking appears to be concentrated in tasks requiring generation of multiple solutions, spatial visualization, and cross-domain connection, rather than in tasks requiring convergent creative evaluation or refinement. The review supports the interpretation that dyslexic cognition is associated with a specific kind of creative capacity — the generative, divergent kind — rather than with creativity as a monolithic trait.

## Key Findings

- **Enhanced divergent thinking.** Multiple studies show that dyslexic individuals generate more ideas, more unusual ideas, and more varied ideas than non-dyslexic controls on divergent thinking tasks (e.g., alternate uses tasks, figural creativity tasks). The advantage is specifically in divergent production — the generation of multiple possibilities from a single starting point.
- **Spatial and visual creativity.** The divergent thinking advantage is most pronounced in spatial and visual domains — generating novel visual designs, imagining alternative spatial configurations, creating original visual compositions. This is consistent with the documented spatial processing advantages (von Karolyi, Gilger).
- **Not all creativity measures.** The advantage does not extend uniformly to all measures of creativity. On tasks requiring convergent creative thinking (finding the single best solution to a well-defined problem), dyslexic participants do not consistently outperform. On tasks requiring creative evaluation or refinement (selecting the best among generated options), results are mixed. The advantage is in generation, not evaluation.
- **The divergent-specific pattern.** The specificity of the advantage supports the interpretation that dyslexic cognition enhances the divergent, generative phase of creative thinking — the phase where multiple possibilities are explored — rather than creativity as a whole. This maps onto the exploitation-exploration framework: divergent thinking is the creative expression of explorative cognition.
- **Cross-domain connection.** Several studies in the review show enhanced performance on tasks requiring connections between semantically distant concepts — a creative measure that is structurally identical to the associative leaping documented by Eide & Eide (2011) and the long-range connectivity documented by Beaulieu (2014).

## Methodology

Systematic review of empirical studies measuring creativity in dyslexic vs. non-dyslexic populations. Inclusion criteria: studies with diagnosed or assessed dyslexic participants, standardized creativity measures, and appropriate control groups. The review covers studies using the Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking (TTCT), alternate uses tasks, figural creativity measures, and domain-specific creative production tasks. Effect sizes and methodological quality are assessed across studies.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Divergent Default, and #6 — Associative Leaping
**Specific connection:** The review provides meta-analytic support for two of the specification's principles. The enhanced divergent thinking finding directly supports Divergent Default — the principle that dyslexic cognition defaults to generating multiple possibilities before narrowing. The enhanced cross-domain connection finding supports Associative Leaping — the principle that dyslexic cognition connects across domains that sequential processing keeps separate. Critically, the specificity of the advantage (divergent but not convergent) supports the specification's design choice to include Divergent Default as a separate principle rather than merging it with other creative capacities. The generative phase is where the dyslexic advantage lies, not in the evaluative or refinement phases.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** Meta-analytic evidence across multiple studies. Individual studies (von Karolyi, Schneps) document specific advantages. This review synthesizes the creativity literature to show that the pattern holds across studies, populations, and measures — the divergent thinking advantage is a robust finding, not an artifact of individual study design.

## Key Quotes

> "The advantage appears specific to the generative phase of creative thinking. Dyslexic individuals excel at producing possibilities, while the evaluative and refinement phases show more mixed results."

> "The pattern of enhanced divergent thinking in dyslexia is consistent with the neuroimaging evidence for broader activation patterns and enhanced long-range connectivity."

## Limitations

Creativity is notoriously difficult to measure, and standardized creativity tests (e.g., TTCT) have been criticized for measuring only certain aspects of creative capacity. Studies in the review vary in sample size, diagnostic criteria for dyslexia, and creativity measures used, making direct comparison difficult. Publication bias may inflate the reported effect sizes — studies finding no advantage may be less likely to be published. The review does not include neuroimaging data, so the link between creativity measures and neural architecture is inferred from convergent evidence rather than directly demonstrated.
