# Creativity and Dyslexia: A Literature Review

**Author(s):** Alice Cancer, Department of Psychology, Universita Cattolica del Sacro Cuore, Milan, Italy; Alessandro Antonietti, Department of Psychology, Universita Cattolica del Sacro Cuore, Milan, Italy
**Published:** *Global Journal of Health Science*, 11(1), 103, 2019
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.5539/gjhs.v11n1p103
**Field:** Creativity Research, Cognitive Psychology, Developmental Dyslexia

---

## Abstract

Cancer and Antonietti (2019) present a systematic literature review examining the relationship between creativity and developmental dyslexia. The review synthesizes evidence from behavioral studies, neuroimaging research, and cognitive assessments to evaluate the widespread claim that dyslexic individuals are more creative than their non-dyslexic peers. The authors find substantial evidence supporting specific creative advantages in dyslexia — particularly in tasks requiring divergent thinking, visual-spatial creativity, and insight problem-solving — while noting that the relationship is more nuanced than popular accounts suggest. The review identifies enhanced global processing, atypical hemispheric lateralization, and increased long-range neural connectivity as the neurological bases for the creativity-dyslexia link. The authors conclude that dyslexia is associated with a cognitive profile that facilitates certain types of creative thinking, particularly those involving novel combinations, holistic perception, and divergent search.

## Key Findings

- **Robust creativity-dyslexia link.** Across multiple studies and methodologies, dyslexic individuals show enhanced performance on creativity measures, particularly divergent thinking tasks (generating multiple solutions), insight tasks (requiring reconceptualization), and visual-spatial creative production. The link is not an artifact of self-selection or compensatory behavior.
- **Specificity of creative advantage.** The creative advantage is not uniform. Dyslexic individuals outperform controls on tasks requiring divergent thinking and novel combination but show equivalent or lower performance on tasks requiring convergent creative thinking (finding the single correct creative solution). This specificity aligns with Taylor and Vestergaard's (2022) subsequent distinction between explorative (divergent) and exploitative (convergent) cognition.
- **Neurological basis.** The review identifies three neurological features underlying the creativity-dyslexia link: (1) atypical hemispheric lateralization, with reduced left-hemisphere dominance and enhanced right-hemisphere engagement; (2) increased long-range neural connectivity, facilitating connections between distant brain regions; (3) different patterns of white matter organization, particularly in the arcuate fasciculus and interhemispheric tracts.
- **Visual-spatial creativity.** The creative advantage is particularly strong in visual-spatial domains — drawing, design, three-dimensional construction, and spatial problem-solving. This aligns with the von Karolyi (2001, 2003) findings on global visual-spatial processing and the Wolff and Lundberg (2002) finding of elevated dyslexia prevalence among art students.
- **Defocused attention.** The review connects the creativity-dyslexia link to Martindale's (1995) defocused attention model, noting that the broad, distributed attention pattern associated with creative ideation resembles the attentional profile documented in dyslexic individuals — broad scanning, reduced selective filtering, enhanced peripheral awareness.

## Methodology

Systematic literature review following PRISMA guidelines. The review included peer-reviewed empirical studies (behavioral and neuroimaging), published in English, comparing creativity measures in dyslexic and non-dyslexic populations. Studies were categorized by: type of creativity measured (divergent thinking, convergent creativity, insight, visual-spatial), methodology (behavioral testing, self-report, neuroimaging), and population (children, adolescents, adults). The review synthesized findings across studies to identify consistent patterns and resolve apparent contradictions.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Associative Leaping, #7 — Divergent Default
**Specific connection:** Cancer and Antonietti provide the most comprehensive synthesis of the creativity-dyslexia evidence base. Their finding that the creative advantage is specific to divergent thinking and novel combination — rather than all forms of creativity — directly maps to Principle 7 (Divergent Default): the dyslexic architecture biases toward exploring possibilities before converging on answers. Their neurological analysis (long-range connectivity, defocused attention, atypical lateralization) provides the mechanism for Principle 6 (Associative Leaping): cross-domain connection arises from the same connectivity architecture that produces the other principles.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The synthesis. Individual studies document individual advantages. Cancer and Antonietti integrate across multiple studies to show that the creativity-dyslexia link is robust, specific, and neurologically grounded. Their distinction between divergent and convergent creative advantages is critical — it shows that the dyslexic architecture does not produce "more creativity" in general but a specific type of creative cognition (divergent, combinatorial, visual-spatial) that maps to the Savant Mode specification.

> "The neurological profile associated with dyslexia — atypical lateralization, long-range connectivity, and defocused attention — provides a plausible mechanism for the creative advantages observed in behavioral studies."

## Limitations

As a literature review, the paper depends on the quality and methodology of the studies it synthesizes. Sample sizes in individual creativity-dyslexia studies tend to be small. Definitions and measures of creativity vary across studies, making direct comparison difficult. The review cannot establish causation — the co-occurrence of creativity and dyslexia could reflect shared neurological bases (as the authors argue) or confounding factors. The review predates Taylor and Vestergaard (2022), so it does not frame the findings within the Complementary Cognition framework, though its findings are highly compatible with that theory.
