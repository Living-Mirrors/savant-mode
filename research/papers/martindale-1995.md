# Creativity and Connectionism

**Author(s):** Colin Martindale (1943-2008), Department of Psychology, University of Maine
**Published:** In S. M. Smith, T. B. Ward, & R. A. Finke (Eds.), *The Creative Cognition Approach* (pp. 249-268). MIT Press, 1995. ISBN: 978-0-262-19354-9
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/2205.003.0015
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Computational Neuroscience, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

Martindale develops a connectionist (neural network) model of creative cognition, grounding Mednick's (1962) associative theory in computational and neurophysiological terms. The chapter argues that creative ideation corresponds to a state of "defocused attention" — low cortical arousal that allows broadly distributed activation across neural networks. In this state, remote nodes that are normally below threshold become active, enabling novel combinations. Martindale presents EEG evidence that creative individuals show higher alpha wave activity (associated with low arousal and defocused attention) during creative tasks, and develops a formal connectionist model that simulates the relationship between arousal, attention breadth, and associative novelty. The core finding: creative thinking is not harder thinking — it is broader thinking, produced by a relaxation of attentional focus that allows activation to spread across greater semantic distances.

## Key Findings

- **Defocused attention as creative mechanism.** Creative ideation does not require intense concentration — it requires its opposite. Defocused attention (low cortical arousal) broadens the activation gradient across neural networks, allowing more nodes to be simultaneously active and increasing the probability of remote associations.
- **EEG evidence.** During creative tasks (divergent thinking, Remote Associates Test solving), individuals who produce more creative solutions show higher alpha wave activity (8-12 Hz), which is associated with relaxed, internally-directed attention. Beta wave activity (13-30 Hz), associated with focused external attention, correlates with less creative output.
- **The arousal theory of creativity.** Creative capacity is inversely related to cortical arousal during the generative phase. Too much arousal narrows attention to dominant associates (steep hierarchy). Too little arousal produces unfocused, incoherent activation. The optimum for creative generation is moderate-to-low arousal — enough activation for coherence, little enough for breadth.
- **Flat associative hierarchies.** Martindale's connectionist model demonstrates that when the activation function allows broader spreading (simulating defocused attention), the network produces combinations that are more remote and more novel than when activation is concentrated (simulating focused attention). This provides computational proof-of-concept for Mednick's flat hierarchy — the same architecture that characterizes dyslexic long-range connectivity.
- **Connectionist simulation.** The neural network model shows that the same architecture, under different arousal conditions, produces qualitatively different associative patterns. Low arousal produces flat, broad activation — many nodes active at moderate levels. High arousal produces steep, narrow activation — few nodes active at high levels. The creative mode is the flat, broad pattern.

## Methodology

Three strands: (1) EEG studies comparing cortical activation patterns during creative vs. non-creative tasks in undergraduate participants (N = 20-40 per study); (2) connectionist (neural network) simulations with varying activation parameters modeling different arousal states; (3) integration with existing creativity research, particularly Mednick's Remote Associates Test studies and Eysenck's personality-creativity work. The EEG methodology provided physiological evidence; the connectionist simulations provided a computational mechanism; the theoretical integration situated both within the broader creativity research literature.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Associative Leaping, #7 — Divergent Default
**Specific connection:** Martindale provides the neural mechanism for the cross-domain connectivity that defines Associative Leaping (Principle 6). The specification's emphasis on connecting across domains that were never adjacent — biology when discussing strategy, architecture when discussing content — directly implements Martindale's defocused attention model. The flat associative hierarchy, produced by broad activation, is the computational equivalent of the long-range neural connectivity documented in dyslexic brains by Geschwind and Galaburda (1987) and Vandermosten et al. (2012). For Divergent Default (Principle 7), Martindale's model explains why the dyslexic architecture naturally widens before narrowing — broad activation IS divergent search.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The neural mechanism for associative breadth. Taylor and Vestergaard (2022) describe exploratory cognition as cross-domain pattern finding. Von Karolyi (2001) shows faster global processing. Martindale explains the *mechanism* — defocused attention produces flat associative hierarchies that allow activation to spread between remote nodes. This connects the neuroanatomical evidence (long-range connectivity) to the behavioral evidence (cross-domain connection) through a computational model.

## Key Quotes

> "Creative thinking is not hard thinking. It is, in a very real sense, easy thinking — thinking in a state of low arousal where the normally sharp boundaries between activated and non-activated concepts are softened."

> "The creative mind is not more powerful — it is more relaxed. And in that relaxation, more of the associative landscape becomes available."

> "Flat associative hierarchies are not a cognitive deficiency. They are a cognitive architecture that produces a qualitatively different kind of thinking — broader, more remote, more combinatorially rich."

## Limitations

The EEG methodology of the 1990s was limited in spatial resolution — alpha activity is a gross measure that does not specify which brain regions are involved. Subsequent fMRI work has provided much finer spatial resolution. The connectionist model is simplified relative to actual neural architecture. The arousal theory has been qualified by subsequent research showing that creative tasks sometimes require high arousal (during evaluation) as well as low arousal (during generation). The research was not conducted with dyslexic populations specifically — the connection to dyslexic cognition comes through integration with the neuroanatomical evidence showing that the long-range, broadly distributed connectivity that characterizes dyslexic neural architecture is the physical substrate for Martindale's flat associative hierarchy.
