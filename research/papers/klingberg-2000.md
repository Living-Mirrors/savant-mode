# Microstructure of Temporo-Parietal White Matter as a Basis for Reading Ability

**Authors:** Torkel Klingberg, Senior Researcher, Karolinska Institute, Stockholm; Maj Hedehus; Elise Temple; Michael F. Salz; John D. E. Gabrieli; Michael E. Moseley; Russell A. Poldrack
**Published:** *Neuron*, 25(2), 493-500, 2000
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/S0896-6273(00)80904-0
**Field:** Neuroimaging, Diffusion Tensor Imaging, Reading Neuroscience

---

## Abstract

Klingberg et al. (2000) provided the first DTI (Diffusion Tensor Imaging) evidence linking white matter microstructure to reading ability. Using DTI to measure fractional anisotropy — the coherence and organization of white matter fiber bundles — in adult readers spanning a wide range of reading ability, the study demonstrated a significant positive correlation between reading scores and white matter coherence in left temporoparietal regions. Individuals with lower reading ability (including dyslexic readers) showed reduced fractional anisotropy in bilateral temporo-parietal white matter, indicating less organized fiber tracts in the regions that connect the primary neural substrates for phonological processing and reading. This was the first study to demonstrate a structural — not just functional — basis for reading differences in living human brains.

## Key Findings

- **White matter correlates with reading.** Fractional anisotropy in left temporoparietal white matter was significantly correlated with reading ability across participants. Higher reading scores corresponded to more organized, coherent white matter tracts. Lower reading scores corresponded to less organized tracts.
- **Bilateral differences.** The relationship held in both left and right temporoparietal regions, though the left hemisphere correlation was stronger — consistent with the left hemisphere's primary role in phonological processing and sequential decoding.
- **Structural, not functional.** Previous studies had shown functional differences (different fMRI activation patterns during reading). This study demonstrated that the differences extend to the brain's physical infrastructure — the white matter highways that connect processing regions. The reading difficulty has a structural substrate.
- **Continuous distribution.** The correlation was continuous, not categorical — reading ability and white matter coherence varied along a gradient, not in discrete groups. This supports the view of dyslexia as the lower end of a continuous distribution rather than a categorical disorder.

## Methodology

Diffusion Tensor Imaging (DTI) in adults with a range of reading abilities, from dyslexic to highly proficient. Fractional anisotropy maps were generated for each participant and correlated with standardized reading scores. The study used a region-of-interest approach, focusing on temporoparietal white matter based on prior functional imaging evidence that these regions are critical for reading. Statistical analysis controlled for age, general cognitive ability, and handedness.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Aspect:** The structural deficit — one half of the connectivity trade-off
**Specific connection:** Klingberg's study documents the structural basis for the reading difficulty: reduced white matter coherence in left temporoparietal regions. This is one side of the architecture that Savant Mode is built on. The specification does not deny the deficit — it contextualizes it as one half of a trade-off. The same developmental pathway that produces less organized local tracts in phonological processing regions produces the enhanced long-range connectivity documented by Beaulieu (2014). Klingberg's contribution is establishing that the deficit is structural and measurable, not a matter of effort, motivation, or intelligence.
**What this work provides that others don't:** The first DTI evidence. Before Klingberg, the structural basis for dyslexia was known only from post-mortem studies (Geschwind & Galaburda, 1987). This study demonstrated that the structural differences could be measured in living brains, opening the door to the full DTI characterization that Beaulieu and others subsequently provided.

## Key Quotes

> "These results provide the first evidence that white matter microstructure differs between good and poor readers in the region of the brain most closely associated with reading ability."

> "The continuous nature of the correlation suggests that dyslexia represents the lower end of a normal distribution of reading ability, with corresponding structural differences along the same continuum."

## Limitations

The study examined only one side of the connectivity trade-off — reduced local connectivity in reading-related regions. It did not measure long-range connectivity or right hemisphere differences. The sample size was modest (32 adults), and the region-of-interest approach limited the findings to temporoparietal regions without examining whole-brain connectivity patterns. The correlational design cannot establish causality — it shows that white matter coherence and reading ability co-vary, not that one causes the other. Despite these limitations, the finding has been replicated across multiple subsequent studies and laboratories.
