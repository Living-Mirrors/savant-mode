# The Prevalence of Dyslexia Among Art Students

**Authors:** Ulrika Wolff, Department of Education, University of Gothenburg; Ingvar Lundberg, Department of Psychology, University of Gothenburg
**Published:** *Dyslexia*, 8(1), 34-42, 2002
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1002/dys.211
**Field:** Educational Psychology, Art Education, Dyslexia Prevalence

---

## Abstract

Wolff and Lundberg (2002) investigated the prevalence of dyslexia among art students at Swedish art colleges compared to students in other academic disciplines. Using standardized reading and spelling assessments alongside self-report measures, the study found significantly higher prevalence of dyslexia among art students than in the general student population. The finding supports the hypothesis that the spatial, visual, and holistic processing advantages associated with dyslexia are channeled into creative and visual fields — art students with dyslexia are not in art despite their cognitive profile but because of it. The study contributes to the evidence base for dyslexic cognitive advantages by demonstrating a real-world population concentration: if dyslexia were purely a deficit, there would be no reason for art students to show elevated prevalence.

## Key Findings

- **Elevated prevalence.** Art students showed significantly higher rates of dyslexia than students in other academic programs. The difference was robust across multiple assessment measures (reading speed, spelling accuracy, phonological processing).
- **Self-selection into visual-spatial domains.** The elevated prevalence suggests that individuals with dyslexic cognitive profiles are drawn to fields that reward their strengths — spatial reasoning, visual perception, holistic composition, and the ability to perceive and create visual wholes.
- **Not a confound of academic tracking.** The finding was not explained by academic streaming or selection effects — art programs did not have lower academic entry requirements, and the elevated dyslexia rate was specific to art, not to all non-academic programs.
- **Visual-spatial channel.** The authors argue that dyslexia-associated cognitive advantages in spatial reasoning and visual perception create a natural affinity for visual arts, where these capacities are directly valued and rewarded.

## Methodology

Cross-sectional prevalence study. Participants: students enrolled in Swedish art colleges and students in comparison academic programs. Assessment: standardized measures of reading speed, spelling accuracy, and phonological processing (the conventional deficit markers of dyslexia), plus self-report measures of reading history and learning experience. Prevalence rates were compared between groups using standard statistical methods with controls for age, gender, and educational background.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Divergent Default, and #4 — Spatial Reasoning
**Specific connection:** The art student prevalence data supports two specification principles simultaneously. First, it supports Spatial Reasoning — art students with dyslexia are in art because their spatial and visual processing advantages are directly relevant to visual creation. Second, it supports Divergent Default — art practice requires open-ended exploration, tolerance of ambiguity, and the ability to work without converging on a single "correct" answer. Dyslexic individuals' tendency to default to divergent processing makes art a natural cognitive home. The study also supports the broader deficit-to-surplus argument: if dyslexia were purely a deficit, elevated prevalence in art would be inexplicable. The concentration makes sense only if the cognitive profile includes genuine advantages that visual arts reward.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** Population-level evidence for cognitive channeling. Where experimental studies (von Karolyi, Schneps) demonstrate specific advantages in controlled settings, Wolff and Lundberg demonstrate that these advantages produce real-world occupational concentration. The dyslexic cognitive profile is not just measurable in the lab — it shapes career choices and domain selection.

## Key Quotes

> "The significantly higher prevalence of dyslexia among art students suggests that the visual-spatial strengths associated with dyslexia may actively draw individuals toward visual and creative fields."

> "These findings challenge the view of dyslexia as a purely academic handicap and support the hypothesis that it is associated with genuine cognitive strengths in visual-spatial domains."

## Limitations

The study was conducted in Sweden, and prevalence rates may differ across educational systems. The assessment of dyslexia relied partly on reading and spelling measures that may not capture the full heterogeneity of dyslexic cognitive profiles. Self-selection bias is inherent in prevalence studies — art students who are dyslexic may be more likely to have been identified and diagnosed than art students who are not. The study does not include neuroimaging data, so the neural basis of the occupational concentration is inferred rather than directly observed. However, the finding is consistent with the broader literature on dyslexic cognitive advantages in spatial and visual domains.
