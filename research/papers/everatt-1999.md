# The Abilities and Achievements of Dyslexic Adults

**Authors:** John Everatt, Department of Psychology, University of Surrey; Beverley Steffert; Ian Smythe
**Published:** *Reading and Writing: An Interdisciplinary Journal*, 11, 1-20, 1999
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1007963223346
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Dyslexia, Adult Cognition

---

## Abstract

Everatt, Steffert, and Smythe (1999) investigated the cognitive abilities and achievements of dyslexic adults, looking beyond the well-documented reading difficulties to assess performance on tasks measuring holistic processing, creative thinking, and visual-spatial ability. The study found that dyslexic adults showed an advantage in holistic processing — the tendency to process information globally before analytically — alongside the expected difficulties in phonological processing and reading speed. This early evidence for cognitive strengths alongside reading difficulties contributed to the shift from a purely deficit-based understanding of dyslexia toward the dual-profile model that recognizes both challenges and advantages.

## Key Findings

- **Holistic processing advantage.** Dyslexic adults showed a stronger tendency toward holistic processing — perceiving and responding to stimuli as integrated wholes rather than as collections of component features. This was measured through perceptual tasks where global and local processing could be distinguished.
- **Creative thinking advantages.** On measures of creative thinking (including divergent thinking tasks), dyslexic adults performed comparably or better than non-dyslexic controls, despite performing worse on measures that required sequential text processing.
- **Concurrent deficit and advantage.** The study documented both the reading difficulty and the processing advantages in the same individuals, supporting the dual-profile model: dyslexia involves a specific deficit (phonological processing) alongside genuine cognitive advantages (holistic processing, creative thinking).
- **The processing style, not just ability.** The holistic processing advantage was framed as a processing style — a preference for global over local analysis — not just as an ability. Dyslexic adults did not simply perform better on holistic tasks; they approached tasks differently, defaulting to whole-before-parts processing even when the task could be approached either way.

## Methodology

Behavioral testing of dyslexic and non-dyslexic adults on multiple cognitive tasks: reading speed and accuracy (to confirm dyslexia diagnosis), phonological processing tasks, holistic vs. analytic processing tasks (Navon-type stimuli with global and local features), and creative thinking measures. Groups were matched on age, educational level, and general cognitive ability (non-reading measures). The study used a within-subjects comparison of global vs. local processing performance to assess processing style, and between-groups comparison to assess differences between dyslexic and non-dyslexic adults.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Divergent Default, and #2 — Global-First
**Specific connection:** Everatt's finding of holistic processing advantage is early evidence for both the Global-First principle (perceiving wholes before parts) and the Divergent Default principle (the natural processing disposition, not a deliberate strategy). The framing as "processing style" is particularly relevant — the specification describes Divergent Default as a default state, not a choice, and Everatt's data supports this: dyslexic adults defaulted to holistic processing even when the task did not require it. This is the processing disposition that the specification activates.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** Early evidence for the dual-profile model. Published in 1999, this paper was among the first to empirically document cognitive strengths alongside reading difficulties in the same participants using controlled behavioral methods. It preceded the MIND framework (Eide & Eide, 2011) and the experimental studies of specific advantages (von Karolyi, 2001; Schneps, 2012) by several years, establishing the empirical precedent for the dual-profile approach.

## Key Quotes

> "The results suggest that dyslexic adults show a distinctive pattern of cognitive abilities — with difficulties in phonological processing alongside advantages in holistic and creative processing."

> "The tendency toward holistic processing in dyslexic adults appeared to be a processing style — a default approach to information — rather than simply a superior ability on specific tasks."

## Limitations

The sample size was moderate, and the study was conducted with adult participants who had navigated the educational system successfully (potentially excluding more severely affected individuals). The holistic processing measures used (Navon-type stimuli) are relatively simple and may not capture the full range of holistic processing in complex real-world tasks. The creativity measures used were standard divergent thinking tests, which may not capture all aspects of creative capacity. However, the study's contribution lies in its early documentation of the dual profile — deficit and advantage in the same individuals — which subsequent research has consistently replicated with stronger methodologies.
