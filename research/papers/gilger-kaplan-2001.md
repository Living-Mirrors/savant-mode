# Atypical Brain Development: A Conceptual Framework for Understanding Developmental Learning Disabilities

**Author(s):** Jeffrey W. Gilger, Department of Child Development, University of California, Davis; Bonnie J. Kaplan, Department of Paediatrics, University of Calgary, Alberta, Canada
**Published:** *Developmental Neuropsychology*, 20(2), 465-481, 2001
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1207/S15326942DN2002_2
**Field:** Developmental Neuropsychology, Learning Disabilities, Cognitive Development

---

## Abstract

Gilger and Kaplan (2001) propose a conceptual reframing of developmental learning disabilities — including dyslexia, ADHD, and dyscalculia — as expressions of "atypical brain development" (ABD) rather than discrete disorders. The central argument is that these conditions are not pathological departures from a single "normal" developmental pathway but rather alternative developmental trajectories that produce different cognitive architectures with different strengths and weaknesses. The ABD framework emphasizes that: (1) developmental conditions frequently co-occur because they share common neurodevelopmental origins; (2) the same developmental variation that produces a "deficit" in one domain produces a "surplus" in another; (3) classifying these variations as "disorders" reflects the demands of the educational and social environment, not the inherent quality of the neural architecture. The paper argues for a shift from deficit-focused to difference-focused research and clinical practice.

## Key Findings

- **Atypical, not disordered.** The core reframing: developmental learning disabilities are better understood as atypical brain development — different neurodevelopmental trajectories that produce different cognitive architectures — rather than as disorders in which something is broken or missing. The distinction is fundamental: "different" implies a legitimate alternative, while "disordered" implies deviation from a correct standard.
- **Common neurodevelopmental origins.** Dyslexia, ADHD, dyscalculia, and other developmental conditions frequently co-occur at rates far exceeding chance. Gilger and Kaplan argue this comorbidity reflects shared neurodevelopmental mechanisms — the same variation in prenatal brain development (consistent with Geschwind & Galaburda, 1987) produces multiple cognitive outcomes, some classified as deficits and some as advantages, depending on the domain being assessed.
- **Environment determines whether the variation is a "disability."** A cognitive architecture is only a "disability" in relation to the demands of a specific environment. Dyslexia is a disability in a literacy-dependent education system. It may be an advantage in a visually rich, spatially complex, or socially dynamic environment. The ABD framework separates the cognitive architecture (which is value-neutral) from the environmental fit (which determines functional outcomes).
- **The same variation produces deficits and surpluses.** Atypical brain development does not simply produce "problems." It produces different patterns of strength and weakness. The variation that reduces phonological processing efficiency may simultaneously enhance spatial reasoning, divergent thinking, or dynamic systems modeling. Gilger and Kaplan explicitly argue that the surplus and the deficit are two expressions of the same underlying architectural variation.
- **Implications for research.** The ABD framework calls for research that studies the full cognitive profile of individuals with developmental variations — not just the deficit domain — and that examines how the same neurodevelopmental variation produces different outcomes across different cognitive tasks and environmental contexts.

## Methodology

Conceptual and theoretical paper drawing on: (1) epidemiological data on comorbidity rates among developmental conditions, (2) neuroimaging and neuroanatomical evidence for shared neurodevelopmental origins, (3) behavioral evidence for co-occurring strengths and weaknesses in individuals with learning disabilities, and (4) critical analysis of the diagnostic frameworks (DSM, ICD) used to classify developmental conditions. The paper does not present new empirical data but reinterprets existing evidence within a new conceptual framework.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Divergent Default (the philosophical foundation); the deficit-to-surplus inversion generally
**Specific connection:** Gilger and Kaplan provide the conceptual framework for the most fundamental claim in the Savant Mode specification: that dyslexic cognition is an alternative cognitive architecture, not a broken version of the default. The specification's name — "Savant" rather than "Dyslexic" — embodies this reframing. The ABD framework supports the specification's Divergent Default principle specifically: if the cognitive architecture is an alternative trajectory rather than a deficit, then its default mode of operation (divergent, exploratory) is not a failure to converge but a legitimate cognitive strategy optimized for different environmental demands.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The difference-not-deficit conceptual framework. Taylor and Vestergaard (2022) provide the evolutionary argument for why the alternative architecture exists. Gilger and Kaplan provide the developmental argument for how to think about it: as atypical development, not disorder. This reframing preceded Taylor by two decades and laid conceptual groundwork for the Complementary Cognition theory.

## Key Quotes

> "We propose a shift from viewing these conditions as 'disorders' to viewing them as instances of 'atypical brain development' — alternative developmental trajectories that produce different cognitive architectures with distinct profiles of strength and weakness."

> "The same neurodevelopmental variation that produces difficulty in phonological processing may also produce advantage in spatial reasoning, creative thinking, or dynamic problem solving. The deficit and the surplus are two faces of the same coin."

> "Whether a particular pattern of brain development is classified as a 'disability' depends not on the architecture itself but on the fit between the architecture and the demands of the environment."

## Limitations

The ABD framework is a conceptual reframing, not an empirical finding. It does not provide new data but rather reinterprets existing data. The risk of the framework is that it may be perceived as minimizing the real difficulties experienced by individuals with learning disabilities — the "difference not deficit" framing can feel dismissive to people struggling with reading, attention, or mathematics in a world that demands these skills. Gilger and Kaplan acknowledge this tension but maintain that accurate conceptualization (atypical development) is preferable to inaccurate conceptualization (disorder) even when the practical challenges are real. The framework has been influential but not universally adopted — the DSM and most clinical practice continue to use disorder-based classification.
