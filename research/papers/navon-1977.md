# Forest Before Trees: The Precedence of Global Features in Visual Processing

**Author(s):** David Navon, Department of Psychology, University of Haifa, Israel
**Published:** *Cognitive Psychology*, 9(3), 353-383, 1977
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0010-0285(77)90012-3
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Visual Perception, Psychophysics

---

## Abstract

Navon (1977) presents a series of experiments establishing the "global precedence effect" — the finding that the global structure of a visual stimulus is processed before its local components. Using hierarchical letter stimuli (large letters composed of smaller letters — e.g., a large H made of small Ss), Navon demonstrated three key findings: (1) global forms are identified faster than local components, (2) global-level information interferes with local-level processing but not vice versa, and (3) the global structure is available for processing before local details regardless of whether attention is directed globally or locally. These findings establish that human visual perception operates on a global-to-local gradient — the forest is seen before the trees. The paper introduced the "Navon letter" paradigm, which became one of the most widely used experimental tools in research on visual attention, object recognition, and hemispheric lateralization.

## Key Findings

- **Global precedence.** When presented with hierarchical stimuli, participants identified the global form (the large letter) faster than the local components (the small letters composing it). This speed advantage for global processing was robust across multiple experiments and stimulus conditions.
- **Global-to-local interference asymmetry.** When the global and local levels conveyed conflicting information (e.g., a large H made of small Ss, where participants must identify either the H or the S), global information interfered with local identification but local information did not interfere with global identification. This asymmetry demonstrates that global processing has priority access — it proceeds automatically and influences local processing, while local processing does not influence global processing.
- **Temporal priority.** Global features are available for processing earlier in time than local features. This is not merely an attentional preference — it reflects the temporal order of information extraction from visual stimuli.
- **The "forest before trees" principle.** Navon's summary formulation: the visual system extracts the overall configuration (the forest) before processing the individual elements (the trees). This is a fundamental architectural feature of human perception, not a strategic choice.

## Methodology

Series of behavioral experiments using hierarchical letter stimuli ("Navon letters"). Participants viewed large letters composed of small letters and performed identification tasks at either the global or local level. Key manipulations: (1) congruent stimuli (global and local levels convey the same letter), (2) incongruent stimuli (global and local levels convey different letters), (3) neutral stimuli (one level is a non-letter shape). Dependent variables: response time and accuracy. The interference paradigm was critical — by measuring whether conflicting global information slowed local identification (and vice versa), Navon could establish the direction of processing priority.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** #2 — Global-First Processing
**Specific connection:** Navon's global precedence effect is the foundational cognitive science behind Principle 2. The specification states: "See the whole before any part. The gestalt arrives before the details." This is a direct implementation of Navon's finding. The principle does not ask the AI to try to see the big picture — it implements a processing order (global before local) that reflects a documented feature of human visual cognition. For dyslexic cognition specifically, the global precedence effect is amplified: enhanced right-hemisphere development (Geschwind & Galaburda, 1987) and right-hemisphere lateralization of global processing (Robertson & Lamb, 1991) produce stronger global-first processing in dyslexic individuals, as confirmed by von Karolyi (2001, 2003).
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The foundational processing principle. Von Karolyi (2001) showed that dyslexic individuals are *faster* at global processing. Navon (1977) established that global processing comes *first* in human perception — it is the default processing order, not a strategy. Combining Navon's temporal finding with von Karolyi's speed finding produces the full picture: global processing is both the default order and amplified in dyslexic cognition.

## Key Quotes

> "The forest is seen before the trees. Global features of a visual scene are processed prior to local features."

> "The advantage of the global level over the local level in speed of processing suggests that global features enjoy a temporal priority — they are available to the cognitive system before local features."

> "Interference from the global level to the local level, but not vice versa, demonstrates that global processing has a privileged status in the perceptual hierarchy."

## Limitations

The Navon letter paradigm, while highly influential, uses a specific class of stimuli (hierarchical letters) that may not generalize to all visual scenes. Natural scenes have more complex hierarchical structure than letters-composed-of-letters. The global precedence effect can be modulated by stimulus factors (relative size of global and local elements, spatial frequency content, eccentricity) and attentional factors (directed attention can partially override global precedence). The research was conducted with neurotypical participants and does not directly address dyslexia — the connection to dyslexic cognition requires integration with neuroanatomical evidence (Geschwind & Galaburda) and behavioral studies of dyslexic populations (von Karolyi).
