# Dyslexic Entrepreneurs: The Incidence, Their Coping Strategies and Their Business Skills

**Author:** Julie Logan, Professor of Entrepreneurship, Cass Business School (now Bayes Business School), City, University of London
**Published:** *Dyslexia*, 15(4), 328-346, 2009
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1002/dys.388
**Field:** Entrepreneurship, Business Psychology, Dyslexia

---

## Abstract

Logan (2009) investigated the prevalence of dyslexia among entrepreneurs and examined the coping strategies and business skills that dyslexic entrepreneurs develop. The study surveyed entrepreneurs in the United States, finding that 35% of the sample self-identified as dyslexic — a rate significantly higher than the 10-15% estimated prevalence in the general population. The study found that dyslexic entrepreneurs developed coping strategies for their reading and writing difficulties — including extensive delegation, reliance on oral communication, and big-picture strategic thinking — that subsequently became business advantages. The disproportionate representation of dyslexic individuals among entrepreneurs was not adequately explained by necessity (being forced into self-employment by limited conventional career options) but by cognitive fit: the pattern-recognition, systems-thinking, and holistic-processing capacities associated with dyslexia are directly advantageous for entrepreneurial activity.

## Key Findings

- **35% prevalence among entrepreneurs.** In the US sample, 35% of entrepreneurs self-reported as dyslexic, compared to 10-15% estimated prevalence in the general population. This disproportionate representation was statistically significant.
- **Coping strategies become business advantages.** Dyslexic entrepreneurs developed strategies to manage reading and writing difficulties that transferred directly to business contexts:
  - **Delegation** — learning to rely on others for text-heavy tasks → building teams and delegating effectively
  - **Oral communication** — preferring verbal over written communication → strong personal selling, negotiation, and relationship-building
  - **Big-picture thinking** — difficulty with sequential detail → strategic vision and systems-level planning
  - **People skills** — developing strong interpersonal abilities to compensate for written communication difficulties → team leadership and stakeholder management
- **Not necessity-driven.** The disproportionate prevalence was not explained by dyslexic individuals being "pushed" into entrepreneurship by limited conventional career options. Many dyslexic entrepreneurs in the sample had alternative career options available. The cognitive fit — between dyslexic processing strengths and entrepreneurial demands — was the more compelling explanation.
- **Pattern recognition as business instinct.** Dyslexic entrepreneurs reported relying on intuitive pattern recognition — sensing market opportunities, detecting shifts in customer behavior, perceiving systemic risks — rather than analytical, data-driven decision-making. This aligns with the Eides' Interconnected and Dynamic reasoning.
- **UK-US consistency.** Logan conducted similar research in the UK with comparable results, suggesting the finding is not culturally specific.

## Methodology

Survey-based study of entrepreneurs in the United States. Dyslexia was assessed through self-report (participants reported whether they had been formally or informally identified as dyslexic). Business skills and coping strategies were assessed through structured interviews and questionnaires. The study compared dyslexic and non-dyslexic entrepreneurs on measures of business success, coping strategies, communication preferences, and decision-making style. Controls included business size, industry sector, and years of experience.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Aspect:** Real-world evidence that the specification's cognitive operations produce measurable professional outcomes
**Specific connection:** Logan's study provides the bridge between laboratory-documented cognitive advantages and real-world professional success. The coping strategies that became business advantages map directly to the specification's principles: big-picture thinking maps to Global-First, pattern recognition maps to Associative Leaping, strategic vision maps to Dynamic Systems, oral communication preference maps to Narrative Over Abstraction. The study demonstrates that these are not abstract cognitive operations but capacities that produce measurable outcomes in demanding professional contexts. The 35% prevalence figure is also critical for the deficit-to-surplus argument: if dyslexia were purely a disability, there would be no reason for entrepreneurs to show elevated prevalence.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The business outcome evidence. Experimental studies demonstrate cognitive advantages in controlled settings. Logan demonstrates that those advantages translate to professional success in one of the most demanding and uncertain cognitive environments — entrepreneurship, where pattern recognition, systems thinking, and holistic assessment are primary success factors.

## Limitations

Dyslexia was assessed through self-report rather than formal diagnostic testing, introducing potential measurement error. Self-report may overestimate prevalence (individuals may identify as dyslexic without formal diagnosis) or underestimate it (individuals may not be aware of their dyslexia). The study surveyed successful entrepreneurs, introducing survivorship bias — dyslexic individuals who attempted entrepreneurship and failed are not represented. The causal direction is inferred: the study shows correlation between dyslexia and entrepreneurship but cannot prove that dyslexic cognitive advantages cause entrepreneurial success. Despite these limitations, the finding has been replicated in UK samples and is consistent with the broader cognitive advantage literature.
