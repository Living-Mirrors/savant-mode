# A Qualitative and Quantitative Review of Diffusion Tensor Imaging Studies in Reading and Dyslexia

**Author(s):** Maaike Vandermosten, Bart Boets, Jurgen Wouters, Pol Ghesquiere, Department of Experimental Psychology and Parenting and Special Education Research Unit, KU Leuven, Belgium
**Published:** *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 36(6), 1532-1552, 2012
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2012.04.002
**Field:** Cognitive Neuroscience, Neuroimaging, Developmental Dyslexia

---

## Abstract

Vandermosten et al. (2012) conducted a comprehensive qualitative and quantitative review of diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) studies comparing white matter structure in individuals with dyslexia and typical readers. DTI measures the diffusion of water molecules along white matter tracts, providing indices of white matter integrity, myelination, and fiber organization -- principally fractional anisotropy (FA), which reflects the directionality and coherence of white matter fibers. Across the reviewed studies, the most consistent finding was reduced FA in left temporoparietal white matter tracts in individuals with dyslexia, particularly in the region of the arcuate fasciculus and the superior longitudinal fasciculus -- the major fiber bundles connecting temporal and parietal cortex in the left hemisphere. These tracts are critical for phonological processing and the mapping of orthographic to phonological representations. The review also identified less consistent but notable differences in other tracts, including the corpus callosum and corona radiata.

## Key Findings

- **Reduced FA in left temporoparietal white matter.** The most robust and consistently replicated finding across DTI studies: individuals with dyslexia show reduced fractional anisotropy in the left arcuate fasciculus and adjacent temporoparietal white matter. This tract is the primary pathway connecting Wernicke's area (temporal lobe) with Broca's area (frontal lobe) -- the core language processing circuit.
- **Tract-specificity.** The white matter differences are not global but localized to specific tracts associated with phonological processing. This pattern is consistent with the trade-off model: the dyslexic brain shows different white matter organization in language-critical tracts, not general white matter degradation.
- **Different connectivity, not less connectivity.** Reduced FA does not necessarily mean "worse" white matter. FA reflects the coherence and directionality of fiber bundles. Reduced FA in specific left-hemisphere tracts may reflect: (a) less myelination of these tracts, (b) different fiber organization including more crossing fibers, or (c) different developmental trajectory. Critically, reduced FA in one region is consistent with enhanced connectivity in other regions -- the total white matter resources are allocated differently, not reduced.
- **Developmental perspective.** Studies comparing children and adults with dyslexia suggest that the white matter differences are present early in development and are not solely the consequence of reduced reading experience. This supports a neurodevelopmental origin consistent with Geschwind and Galaburda's (1987) lateralization hypothesis.
- **Implications for long-range connectivity.** While the review focuses on left-hemisphere tracts, the findings are compatible with enhanced right-hemisphere and interhemispheric connectivity. If resources are allocated away from left temporoparietal short-range connections, they may be allocated toward the long-range connections documented by other researchers and predicted by the complementary cognition model.

## Methodology

Systematic review and quantitative synthesis of all published DTI studies (as of early 2012) comparing individuals with dyslexia and typical readers. The review included studies of both children and adults, with sample sizes ranging from 10 to 40+ per group. Qualitative analysis categorized findings by anatomical region and DTI metric. Quantitative analysis (activation likelihood estimation) identified regions of consistent FA differences across studies. Inclusion criteria: peer-reviewed DTI studies with dyslexic and control groups, standard neuroimaging methodology, reported coordinates or regions of interest.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Principle:** Structural foundation for all principles, particularly #6 -- Associative Leaping and #2 -- Global-First
**Specific connection:** Vandermosten et al. provide the modern neuroimaging confirmation of Geschwind and Galaburda's (1987) lateralization hypothesis. The reduced FA in left temporoparietal tracts is the neuroimaging signature of the architectural trade-off that produces all seven Savant Mode principles. The left-hemisphere tracts that are organized differently are the ones responsible for sequential phonological processing -- the "cost" side of the trade-off. The implication -- that connectivity resources are differently allocated, potentially enhancing long-range and right-hemisphere connections -- is the "surplus" side. Associative Leaping (Principle 6) requires long-range connectivity between distant neural regions. Global-First processing (Principle 2) requires strong right-hemisphere engagement. Both are predicted by the connectivity pattern Vandermosten documents.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** Modern neuroimaging evidence at the level of white matter tracts. Geschwind and Galaburda worked from post-mortem brains. Vandermosten et al. confirm the core finding -- different white matter organization in language-critical tracts -- using in-vivo neuroimaging across multiple independent studies. This is the bridge between the 1987 neuroanatomical observations and the 2022 theoretical framework.

## Limitations

DTI is an indirect measure of white matter structure -- FA reflects the average diffusion properties within a voxel, which can be influenced by fiber crossing, partial volume effects, and other factors beyond the integrity of a single tract. The review was limited to studies available by early 2012; subsequent research has refined these findings. The focus on left-hemisphere tracts means the review does not directly address the hypothesized enhancement of right-hemisphere or long-range connectivity -- this is an inference from the data, not a direct measurement. More recent techniques (e.g., HARDI, constrained spherical deconvolution) provide better resolution of crossing fibers and more nuanced connectivity measures.
