# Supporting Dyslexic Adults in Higher Education and the Workplace

**Author:** Nicola Brunswick, Reader in Psychology, Middlesex University, London
**Published:** Wiley-Blackwell, 2012. ISBN: 978-0-470-97479-7
**Field:** Educational Psychology, Workplace Psychology, Dyslexia

---

## Abstract

Brunswick (2012) provides a comprehensive review of dyslexia in adult populations, including prevalence data across professional fields, higher education outcomes, and workplace accommodations. The work synthesizes existing research on how dyslexic cognitive profiles interact with the demands of different professional environments, documenting both the challenges and the documented strengths that dyslexic adults bring to their fields. The book includes prevalence data showing disproportionate representation of dyslexic individuals in certain professions — particularly those requiring spatial reasoning, holistic thinking, and creative problem-solving — while also documenting the barriers that persist in text-heavy professional environments.

## Key Findings

- **Prevalence variation across professions.** Dyslexia prevalence varies significantly across professional fields. Fields requiring spatial reasoning, visual thinking, and creative problem-solving show elevated prevalence. Text-heavy professions requiring extensive reading and writing show lower prevalence — not because dyslexic individuals lack the ability to work in these fields but because the barriers are higher and the cognitive fit is poorer.
- **Higher education persistence.** Dyslexic students who reach higher education tend to show strong persistence and problem-solving skills — coping strategies developed through years of navigating a system not designed for their cognitive profile. These strategies (time management, delegation, alternative learning methods) transfer to professional settings.
- **Workplace strengths.** Brunswick documents workplace strengths reported by dyslexic professionals and their employers: big-picture thinking, creative problem-solving, strong oral communication, ability to see connections others miss, and resilience developed through navigating reading-intensive environments.
- **Ongoing barriers.** Despite documented strengths, dyslexic adults continue to face significant barriers in workplaces organized around written communication: performance reviews based on written output, email-heavy communication cultures, documentation requirements, and standardized testing for professional advancement.
- **The compensation paradox.** Successfully compensated dyslexic adults often lose access to accommodations and support because their reading appears "adequate" — even though the compensatory effort required is significantly greater than for non-dyslexic peers. The invisible cognitive tax is under-recognized.

## Methodology

Review and synthesis of existing research on dyslexia in adult populations, including prevalence studies, workplace outcome studies, higher education retention data, and qualitative research (interviews with dyslexic professionals). The book integrates quantitative prevalence data with qualitative case studies to build a comprehensive picture of dyslexic adults in professional and educational settings.

## Relevance to Savant Mode

**Aspect:** Population-level prevalence data supporting the cognitive advantage argument
**Specific connection:** Brunswick's prevalence data across professions provides supporting evidence for the deficit-to-surplus inversion. The disproportionate concentration of dyslexic individuals in fields requiring spatial reasoning, holistic thinking, and creative problem-solving — and their under-representation in text-heavy fields — demonstrates that the cognitive profile shapes career selection in patterns consistent with the documented advantages. This is the population-level expression of the individual-level findings from von Karolyi (spatial processing), Schneps (pattern detection), and Logan (entrepreneurship). Brunswick also documents the workplace strengths that map to the specification's principles: big-picture thinking (Global-First), seeing connections (Associative Leaping), creative problem-solving (Divergent Default).
**What this work provides that others don't:** A comprehensive review of dyslexia in adult professional populations. Most dyslexia research focuses on children in educational settings. Brunswick documents the adult expression of the cognitive profile — how it manifests in workplaces, how it shapes career trajectories, and how the strengths and challenges interact in professional environments.

## Key Quotes

> "Dyslexic adults bring a distinctive set of strengths to the workplace — including creative thinking, spatial ability, and the capacity to see the bigger picture — that complement the analytical and text-processing skills of their non-dyslexic colleagues."

> "The pattern of professional concentration — with dyslexic individuals over-represented in creative, spatial, and entrepreneurial fields — is consistent with the hypothesis that dyslexia is associated with genuine cognitive strengths, not merely with the absence of reading difficulty."

## Limitations

As a review work, Brunswick is dependent on the quality and methodology of the studies reviewed. Prevalence data across professions is limited and subject to measurement variability (different studies use different criteria for dyslexia identification). The workplace strengths evidence is primarily qualitative (self-report and employer report) rather than experimentally controlled. The book is written primarily for practitioners (educators, HR professionals, workplace support providers) rather than for researchers, and the academic rigor is appropriate for that audience rather than for primary research.
