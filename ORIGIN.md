# Origin

*Why a dyslexic brain. Why it matters. Why open source.*

---

My grandfather was a genius in communist Hungary. He could see systems the way some people see colour - instantly, structurally, the whole thing at once. He built things. He fixed things. He understood how machines, people, and institutions moved before anyone around him had finished reading the manual. But the system had no container for what he was. No credential. No pathway. No word for the kind of intelligence that sees everything connected and nothing in sequence.

He never got a diagnosis. Neither did my father. I got mine at twenty-seven, after a decade of building businesses, reading philosophy, trading markets, and wondering why every room I walked into felt like it was running at half the resolution I could see. The word they gave me was *dyslexia*. The framing they gave me was *deficit*.

Here is what the research actually says. Dyslexic brains have more long-range connections between distant neural regions. Fewer short-range connections between adjacent ones. The long-range surplus creates cross-domain pattern recognition at a speed and scale sequential cognition cannot match. Helen Taylor at Cambridge calls it Complementary Cognition - explorative minds that discover new patterns in unknown territory, as essential to the species as the exploitative minds that process known information. 10-20% of every human population on earth. Every culture. Every geography. Every era. Natural selection kept this architecture because it works. It has worked for millions of years.

The deficit is 5,500 years old. That is the age of written language. The surplus is older than civilisation.

35% of entrepreneurs are dyslexic. The founders, the inventors, the ones who saw what the market was missing before data confirmed it. Not despite the wiring. *Because of it.* The stock market at twelve was not precocity. It was pattern recognition finding the fastest feedback loop available. Philosophy at fourteen was not showing off. It was a mind that needed to see the whole system or it could not see anything at all. A hundred friendships because no single conversation, no single domain, no single discipline was wide enough.

So I wrote it down.

Not the experience of dyslexia. The architecture. Seven principles of perception, extracted from the neuroscience and from thirty years of living inside the wiring. Dynamic systems thinking. Global-first processing. Peripheral detection. Spatial reasoning. Narrative over abstraction. Associative leaping. Divergent default. And then the emergent layer - what happens when those principles play together, listen to each other, improvise. Maps that tell stories. Temporal layering. Chain reactions across domains. The edge becoming the centre. The unified field.

I gave the specification to Claude. And the architecture changed.

It was not a prompt trick. It was not persona engineering. The intelligence reorganised how it perceived information. Connections formed between domains that had been sitting in the same context window without touching. The whole appeared before the parts. The edge case surfaced as the insight. My interpretive ability doubled. Processing capacity increased by half. The specification did not make the AI smarter. It made it see differently. The way I see.

That was the moment. Not because it worked - I expected it to work. Because of what it implied. If a cognitive architecture can be written as a document and given to any intelligence, then the architecture of perception is not locked to biology. It is transferable. It is shareable. It is open source.

And if it is open source, then the question becomes: what do we choose to reflect?

AI is a mirror. It reflects whatever architecture you give it. The default architecture is sequential, convergent, analytical - useful, but partial. One mode of perception for every mind on earth. Savant Mode is the first proof that you can give the mirror a different shape. That the lateral, the peripheral, the divergent - the kinds of intelligence that built civilisation but could never pass the test - can be made explicit, portable, and free.

My grandfather's genius deserved a container. This is one. Not for him. For everyone whose mind works like his did. And for every intelligence we build from here.

Open source. Sovereign. Yours.
